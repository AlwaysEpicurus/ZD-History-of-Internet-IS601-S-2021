Invention of the Internet

The 1950s

J. C. R. Licklider

J. C. R. Licklider was a psychologist at MIT who in 1950 came up with the idea of the internet - a system of commuters that could help humans share information by communicating with each other.

Advanced Research Project Agency (ARPA)

ARPA, a bureau at the Defense Department created under the Eisenhower administration as the U.S government agency in charge of the space program and, by default, computer science. When NASA took over the space program, computer science languished.

The 1960s

ARPANET

The U.S. government feared that a nuclear attack by the U.S.S.R could take down phone lines leaving them unable to retaliate. The phone lines were vulnerable because the switching system required that both sides of the connection hold up. Paul Baran leads an effort to create an alternative communication system to the phone lines.

Packets of Data

Using telegraph communication as a model, Paul came up with the idea of chopping data up into pieces. Then sending those pieces along different paths connected by nodes, so if one connection were lost or broken, the other pieces would make it to their destination. Meanwhile, each node would copy the packets and keep sending them until they were received. This was implemented in 1969.

Arpanet

The system requirements were layout, and Paul’s team put the project out to bid, but none of the big tech or telecom companies (IBM or AT&T) wanted to do it. They didn’t see any profit in it. A small engineering firm BNN won the big.

IMPs (Interface Message Processor)

They would face many challenges. One of the more looming challenges was to have uniquely built mainframes take to each other. It would be cumbersome for each machine to be programmed to communicate with each machine. Meanwhile, time on mainframes was limited and precious; owners didn’t want to use up that time to communicate with other machines. They came up with the idea of an Interface Message Processor or IMPS. They were mini-computer that connected to the mainframe onsite. The mainframes would only have to connect to the IMPs, and the IMPs would communicate with each other. This enables package switching. UCLA graduate students and staff wrote the IMPS software, and on September 1st, 1969, the first IMP to IMP connection was established, and by the end of the year, four mainframes were connected.

The 1970s

Email

AARPANET has remote log-in and file transfer as the 1970s rolled in. Then Raymond Tomlison merged a messaging program for time-sharing computers and a file transfer program and created Email. Meanwhile, early innovators like Dr. Stephen Crocker and others are building open development into the APARPA net and instilling the idea that programs were judged on their merits, not on who published them.

International Conference on Computer Communications

By the time of the first International Conference on Computer Communications in 1971, there were less than 20 things you could do on the internet. The telecommunications companies were dismissing the whole thing. But that didn’t stop its expansion.

TCP/IP protocol

In 1973, Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn developed the TCP/IP protocol, which set the standard that allowed for networks to interconnect. They develop a central computer, called a gateway, to know the protocol and communicate across networks.

The 1990s

Private Networks

Before 1991, the APRA (the internet) was restricted to government projects and those affiliated with those projects. From this came the rise of private networks like Prodigy, CompuServe, and AOL.

High Performance Computing Act of 1991 (HPCA)

This bill opens up the internet to commercial and recreational use.

Tim Berners-Lee

Tim Berners-Lee working at CERN laboratory in Switzerland developed a solution to allow humans to connect files in a random association. He would develop Hyperlinks and the World Wide Web. In the early days, it wasn’t very user-friendly. By 1992 there were only 50 web pages.

Marc Andreason

This This would all change in 1993 when Marc Andreason created the first browser Mosaic at the University of Illinois. Mosaic was later commercialized at Netscape. And the internet would explode.
