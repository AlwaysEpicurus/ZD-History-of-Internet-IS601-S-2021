The True Story of the Internet

Browser Wars Part 1

The Birth of Mosaic Online

The World Wide Web was a tremendous unknown when it was created in 1989 by an English scientist named Tim Berners-Lee. The World Wide Web consisted of a very few pages with loads of text on it not worth finding , and for the lucky few to use it there was no easy way to be able to find these web pages. This would all change when Marc Andreessen, along with a few of his college friends, decided to change the internet landscape forever by creating the first graphical web browser: Mosaic Online. In the fall of 1993, Andreessen and company released Mosaic Online, which was a freely downloadable gift for all. To create awareness of Mosaic Online, Andreessen decided to make an announcement on a newsnet news group for those interested in being a part of a beta effort. From this announcement, 12 people responded and were sent the first browser. This initial group would grow rapidly as news of Mosaic Online would spread, reaching millions of people within a short timeline.

Netscape comes to being

News of Mosaic Online would quickly reach Jim Clark, a Stanford professor and Co-Founder of Netscape. Clark would approach Andreessen for a business partnership to continue on the success of Mosaic Online, and the duo would soon gather a group of engineers to create Netscape. The creation of Netscape would become worrisome for major tech companies, including Microsoft.

A Threat to Microsoft

Bill Gates, founder and CEO of Microsoft at the time, saw Netscape as a threat to their technological supremacy, since by 1993, about 90% of the world’s PCs were running Microsoft software, and Netscape were cutting into their reign. On top of this, Gates knew that at some point a small startup would come in to the tech landscape and topple Microsoft as the leader in technology/browser supremacy, which Microsoft had done years prior. On October 13th, 1994, Netscape would launch its own web browser for use: Navigator. From here, the internet landscape would change forever, as more and more people would use these web browsers in their daily lives. Navigator would soon challenge Microsoft’s software platform supremacy, and force Gates to rethink the impact the web would have on daily life, and forced Microsoft engineers to fight back.

The Browser Wars Begin

Knowing Microsoft would soon be coming after them, Netscape would hire Gary Reback, an anti-trust lawyer, to represent them in the event Microsoft would do anything illegal. After receiving threats from Microsoft, Reback would soon alert the Department of Justice to look into the practices of Microsoft, which would spell trouble for Microsoft. Soon after, Netscape would go public, further igniting the internet boom. After some inflammatory comments from Andreessen, Microsoft set its sights on their own web browser: Internet Explorer. What would ensue would be a bitter back-and-forth between the two web browsers. Gates would use all Microsoft engineers on Internet Explorer, and would use their businessmen to ensure that all computer manufacturers would ship only Internet Explorer, and prevent installing anything but Internet Explorer. By September of 1997, Netscape would lose the browser wars, but Windows would face another threat: the U.S. government.

Anti-Trust Problems

Gates would face a massive anti-trust lawsuit, being accused of forcing consumers to use Internet Explorer, and no other web browser. After a lengthy trial, and almost having Microsoft being split into two, Gates would pass over the CEO position to Steve Ballmer. However, the impact of these browser wars would later bring on other companies, such as Google.
