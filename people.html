Web 2.0

Digg

Kevin Rose and Jay Adelson Founder It was a news aggregator where users could vote stories up and down. Super-users had weighted votes allowed for a community of super-users to control what became popular. This network of super-users could instigate a Digg Effect variant of the slash-dot effect that would drive massive traffic to a page if it was a top-ranking site on Digg. It caused have ripple effect first from del.icio.us, then later from StumbleUpon (now Mix) It was an attempt to democratize media distribution.

IBM and EMI experimented in 1999 to test if consumers would be willing to purchase music online, download it, and burn it to a CD. They concluded that consumers would not want to do such a thing.

Napster

Shawn Fanning started after seeing his roommate spend countless hours trying to find and download music off the internet. Napster allowed users to share their MP3 collections. Napster was the first program Fanning created He tried to keep it among friends, but 10,000 people were using it within the first week. Four months after, Napster had become the faster spreading software with over a million downloads. Napster would run into a series of legal trouble around copyright entrenchment leading to a judgment that shut them down in July 2001

Myspace

Founded by Christa Wolf and Tom Anderon in 2003 MySpace found early success from music sharing. They had 27 million users by 2005 Became a marketing vehicle Published by News Corp for 640 million bucks.

Facebook

Founded by Mark Zuckerberg It started for Harvard Students then expanded to other universities. Zuckerberg wanted to map out all the relationships people have. Microsoft purchase 1.6% of Facebook for $240 million
